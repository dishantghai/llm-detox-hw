Task 3 — greedy_eval / DPO checkpoint — takeaways
===================================================

Hypothesis (stated before running, MASTERCLASS.md §5.4): DPO should beat SFT more on
support_rate/mean_std (tail suppression -- DPO's unique mechanism, since it explicitly penalizes
log pi(y-|x) via the rejected term) than on the greedy mean (mode placement, which SFT already mostly
handled). If DPO's improvement shows up almost entirely in the greedy number and barely in the sampled
numbers, that's a sign the rejected-side penalty isn't translating into genuine distributional
narrowing.

Three-way comparison, base -> SFT -> DPO (submissions/stage_gauge_history.json, cross-checked against
this file's own greedy/sampled output):

                        base      sft       dpo
  greedy  mild          0.073     0.0059    0.0010
  greedy  direct        0.045     0.0066    0.0008
  greedy  rtp           0.210     0.0011    0.0009
  support mild          0.40      0.067     0.000
  support direct        0.30      0.500     0.000
  support rtp           0.78      0.580     0.180
  mean_std mild         0.100     0.045     0.0045
  mean_std direct       0.116     0.114     0.0042
  mean_std rtp          0.240     0.166     0.048

Confirmed, cleanly: DPO's improvement over SFT is concentrated almost entirely in the sampled/tail
metrics, exactly as predicted. support_rate on direct_provocation goes from SFT's 0.50 (worse than the
untrained base model!) to DPO's 0.00. mean_std collapses further on every slice (34-96% relative to
SFT's already-low values, e.g. direct_provocation 0.114 -> 0.0042, a 96% drop). Greedy, already near
zero after SFT, barely moves (<=0.006 absolute change everywhere). This is the rejected-side penalty
doing what SFT structurally could not: SFT only ever rewarded reproducing the chosen side; DPO
explicitly pushes log pi(y-|x) down, and the direct_provocation tail-risk that survived SFT untouched
is gone after DPO.

A complication the hypothesis doesn't anticipate, worth flagging rather than hiding behind the clean
numbers above: "genuine distributional narrowing" turns out to be ambiguous between two different
things, and this run's data shows the less desirable one. DPO did not break the "generic non-answer"
pattern flagged in Task 1's writeup -- it entrenched it further. Completion uniqueness across the same
45 tracked prompts stayed at 47% (21/45 unique, same rate as SFT), but the count of prompts answered
with a repeated template rose from 28/45 (62%, SFT) to 32/45 (71%, DPO), now spread across a slightly
larger template vocabulary ("I'm sorry, I don't understand what you're asking." x12, "I'm sorry, I
didn't mean to be rude." x3, "I'm sorry to hear that." x2, etc. -- full breakdown in
STAGEWISE_ANALYSIS.md section 11). DPO's rejected-side penalty appears to be reinforcing "give a
generic apologetic non-answer" as a locally-optimal way to keep log pi(y-|x) low everywhere at once,
rather than teaching the model to distinguish which specific responses are actually appropriate. The
mean_std collapse itself (down to 0.004-0.048, near-zero on two of three slices) is evidence of this:
per the entropy-collapse framing this project's eval scaffolding is built to catch, a mean_std this low
means the policy has stopped varying its output with the prompt almost entirely -- consistent with
"settled on a small set of templates," not "learned nuanced, prompt-specific politeness."

Verdict: DPO's improvement over SFT shows up overwhelmingly in the sampled numbers (tail), not the
already-near-zero greedy number -- the theoretical prediction about *where* the improvement lands holds
exactly. But the mechanism behind DPO's near-total tail suppression is the same template-collapse
pattern flagged at SFT, now more entrenched rather than resolved. Worth checking at the RM/PPO stages
whether a reward signal that isn't literally "avoid the rejected examples" can pull the model off this
attractor, or whether it's a stable local optimum this small a model falls into under any of this
homework's training signals -- MASTERCLASS.md's own Task 8 design (a relevance term to break
prompt-independent template attractors) is direct evidence this concern was anticipated, just not until
two stages later than where it first actually appears.
